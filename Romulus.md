# Romulus — First King of Rome

**Romulus** is the legendary founder and first king of [[Rome]], traditionally
dated to **753 BC**. With his twin brother **Remus**, he stands at the head of
Roman myth — and at the head of the list of the seven [[Kings of Rome]].

## The twins

Romulus and Remus were sons of **Rhea Silvia**, a Vestal Virgin, and the god
**Mars**. Ordered drowned in the Tiber by the usurper **Amulius**, the infants
were instead suckled by a **she-wolf** (the *Lupa*) and raised by the shepherd
**Faustulus**. The she-wolf nursing the twins became one of Rome's enduring
emblems.

## Founding of the city

On reaching adulthood the twins resolved to found a city on the banks of the
Tiber, but quarrelled over its site and name:

- Romulus favoured the **Palatine Hill**.
- Remus favoured the **Aventine Hill**.

They sought the will of the gods through **augury**. The dispute turned violent
and **Remus was killed** — by Romulus himself, in most tellings, after leaping
the half-built walls in mockery. Romulus named the city **Roma** after himself
and became its first king.

## Reign

Tradition credits Romulus with the founding institutions of the city:

- The **Senate** — an advisory council of 100 elders (*patres*), origin of the
  *patricians*.
- The **legions** and the division of citizens into military units.
- The **asylum** on the Capitoline, which drew settlers to the new city.
- The **Rape of the Sabine Women** — a stratagem to secure wives for Rome's men,
  leading to war and then union with the Sabines under **Titus Tatius**.

## Death and deification

After a reign of some 37 years, Romulus is said to have **vanished during a
storm** on the Campus Martius. The Romans came to believe he had been taken up
to the gods and worshipped him as **Quirinus**, a deity of the Roman state.

## Legacy

Whether historical or wholly legendary, Romulus framed how Rome told its own
origins: a city born of divine descent, fratricidal struggle, and martial
institution-building. The founding date **753 BC** became the anchor of the
Roman calendar — *ab urbe condita*, "from the founding of the city."
